First Impressions and Unboxing

When I first received the Sierra Designs High Side 1, I was immediately struck by its compact packaging. The entire tent, including poles and stakes, arrived in a surprisingly small compression sack measuring just 13 inches long. Having previously used the ***Six Moon Designs Lunar Solo***, I was curious how this traditionally-poled tent would compare to trekking pole shelters.

The build quality immediately caught my attention during my Sierra Designs High Side 1 review process. The silnylon fabric felt substantial yet lightweight, with reinforced stress points at pole attachment locations. The pre-bent aluminum poles, a signature feature of this tent, showed excellent craftsmanship with smooth joints and no sharp edges that might catch on fabric.

Expert Tip: Initial Setup Strategy

During my first setup, I discovered that practicing the pitch sequence at home significantly reduces field setup time. The High Side's unique pole geometry requires a specific order of operations that becomes intuitive after 2-3 practice sessions.

The included stakes deserve special mention in this Sierra Designs High Side 1 review. While functional, the 13 aluminum stakes add considerable weight to the package. I quickly replaced them with titanium alternatives, saving 4.2 ounces - a significant reduction for ultralight enthusiasts. This tent definitely falls into the category requiring multiple stakes, unlike some ***simpler tent designs*** that require fewer anchor points.

Real-World Testing & Performance

My comprehensive Sierra Designs High Side 1 review testing spanned four seasons across diverse environments. From the humid coastal ranges of Oregon to the arid landscapes of Utah's desert country, this tent faced conditions that would challenge any ultralight shelter. The most memorable test came during a late-season storm in the North Cascades, where 40mph winds and horizontal rain provided the ultimate stress test.

Wind Resistance Performance

The low-profile design excels in windy conditions. During that Cascades storm, I watched other campers struggle with their taller shelters while the High Side remained remarkably stable. The pre-bent poles create natural wind-shedding angles, and the multiple guy-out points allow for precise tuning. However, achieving optimal stability requires using 9-11 stakes, significantly more than simpler designs.

Weather Performance Summary

  • Wind: Excellent stability up to 45mph with proper guy-out
  • Rain: Complete waterproofing, minimal seepage at seams
  • Snow: Good load capacity, easy snow shedding
  • Condensation: Moderate buildup in humid conditions

Condensation Management

Condensation proved to be the tent's most challenging aspect during my Sierra Designs High Side 1 review testing. The single-wall sections and relatively small vestibule can lead to moisture buildup during humid nights. I found that the innovative awning configuration helps significantly - by partially opening the vestibule and using the kick-out vents, air circulation improves dramatically.

Compared to my experience with other ultralight options, the condensation performance sits in the middle range. It's notably better than single-wall shelters but doesn't match the ventilation capabilities of larger double-wall designs. Strategic campsite selection and using the various ventilation options became crucial skills for optimal comfort.

Setup Process & Design Features

The Sierra Designs High Side 1 review wouldn't be complete without detailing the setup process, which represents both the tent's greatest strength and its primary learning curve. Unlike trekking pole shelters that can be intuitive, this tent requires understanding its specific sequence for optimal results.

Step-by-Step Setup Sequence

  1. Ground Preparation: Clear and level the site, accounting for the tent's 88-inch length
  2. Inner Tent Deployment: Stake out the four corners, ensuring proper tension
  3. Pole Assembly: Connect the pre-bent poles, noting the asymmetrical design
  4. Body Attachment: Clip the inner tent to poles using color-coded attachments
  5. Fly Installation: Drape carefully, ensuring proper pole pocket alignment
  6. Guy-out System: Secure vestibule and side panels with remaining stakes

The side-entry design deserves special recognition in this Sierra Designs High Side 1 review. After experiencing the cramped head-entry of traditional ultralight tents, the High Side's side zipper felt revolutionary. Midnight bathroom breaks become significantly easier, and the entry process doesn't require the contortionist skills needed for end-entry designs.

Versatile Vestibule Configurations

The High Side's vestibule system offers remarkable flexibility through its dual-zipper design. The standard configuration provides 7.7 square feet of covered storage - adequate for a backpack and boots. However, the real innovation lies in the awning mode, where one or both zippers can create an angled shelter perfect for cooking or simply enjoying views while staying protected.

During my testing, I frequently used the partial awning configuration, which maintains weather protection while dramatically improving ventilation. This feature proved particularly valuable during summer trips when traditional tent designs would feel suffocating. The system works well with trekking poles for extended coverage, though the dedicated tent poles provide adequate support for most conditions.
